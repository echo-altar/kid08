Love Actually

March 27, 2026

Love Actually is an album about love in all its fractures. About the bad exes, the good ones who got away, the ones you waited for too long, and the ones you should never have waited for at all. It is not interested in romance as fantasy, but in love as lived experience: fierce, humiliating, tender, repetitive, and unresolved. Throughout the record, devotion hardens into resentment, intimacy stalls, and clarity arrives only after damage has already been done.

At the center of Kid08 are two characters as much as two musicians. Blueface Adler is the controlled, almost regal vocalist, her blue-painted face fixed in a mixture of sadness and beauty, delivered with confrontational calm. Opposite her stands Sideshow Vid, the eccentric, unpredictable genius: virtuosic, restless, and deliberately excessive. Together, they form a cabaret-punk villain duo, extravagant and exaggerated, equal parts masterminds and cartoon antagonists. These characters are further expanded in a comic written and illustrated by Gabriella Danerlöv, which follows Adler’s transformation into Blueface Adler and her acts of revenge against former lovers, with Vid cast as her scheming villain companion.

Written between 2022 and 2025, the album draws on autobiographical material from Kosman’s love life, filtered through dreams and abstraction. The songs move between different failures and forms of longing. “I Am On to You, Babe” opens the album with blunt accusation and a newfound awareness, refusing reconciliation. “Years Spent Waiting” captures the exhaustion of longing and emotional dependency, while “Letter” lingers in restraint, replaying intimacy internally while refusing to make it real. In “Little (Nothing) in Return,” disappointment accumulates through melodic repetition, as expectation repeatedly outweighs what is given back.

Elsewhere, the album allows for contrast and contradiction. “Easy/Slow” drifts through vulnerability and desire, while “Lonely Glow,” featuring Soap Horse on lap steel and vocals, offers reciprocity through a developing, crescending motif. “Stephanie” collides punk urgency with lush, processed strings and solo by Klara Källström on cello, holding grief and futility side by side. Just before the album’s closure comes “Kärlek egentligen,” a spoken-word poem written and performed by Aya Kanbar, which interrupts the song cycle entirely, stripping love down to concept and contradiction. The last song introduces us to an almost entirely new sound: “Enter,” featuring Sikaï Li and Konrad Angas, abandons pop structure altogether, moving from noise and orchestration into an improvised drum-led finale that opens the album outward to new territory, rather than resolving what has come before it.

The title Love Actually is no stranger to any of us. Whereas in the film it promises reassurance, Kid08 uses it to expose love’s flaws, repetitions, and failures. This is not an album about happy endings, but about seeing love clearly, even when it disappoints. Love Actually is released March 27, 2026, together with the comic, and will be followed by a tour in April with shows in Stockholm, Malmö, and Göteborg.
